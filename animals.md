# Animals

**Summary**: Overview of the source's description of animal traits, embryonic development, and major invertebrate phyla.

**Sources**: raw/Zoology.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-14

---

The source defines animals as heterotrophic organisms marked by irritability, movement, and at least one sexual phase in the life cycle. It emphasizes that animal cells lack the cell walls of plants and fungi and treats nerve cells as one of the main features separating animals from other multicellular forms. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 22-32)

## General animal traits

The book connects the animal way of life to feeding and movement. Animals are presented as organisms that generally move to obtain food, respond actively to external stimuli, and stop growing continuously once adulthood is reached, producing new cells mainly for maintenance and repair. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 22-32)

The source dates the first animals capable of digesting food to roughly 700 million years ago and uses that point as the beginning of the specifically animal branch of its story. From there it builds an overview through anatomy, embryology, and phylum-level comparison. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 22-32)

## Embryonic development

Embryology is one of the main organizing principles of the animal chapters. The source introduces the zygote, blastomeres, blastula, gastrula, and later organogenesis, and it argues that similarities in early development reveal evolutionary kinship even when adult animals look very different. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 22-32)

The distinction between diploblastic and triploblastic animals is especially important in the source. Diploblasts are described as developing from ectoderm and endoderm, while triploblasts add a mesoderm that later gives rise to more complex tissues and organ systems. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 22-32)

## Major invertebrate groups

The progression through phyla is presented as a rise in structural complexity. Porifera are described as simple filter-feeding animals without true tissues, coelenterates as diploblastic animals with radial symmetry and stinging cells, and platyhelminthes as some of the first animals in the book's sequence to show clear organs and bilateral symmetry. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 22-32)

Annelids are used to introduce segmentation and cephalization, while molluscs are organized around the body plan of head, visceral sac, and foot. Echinoderms are characterized by their protective internal plates and radial organization, and arthropods are presented as the major evolutionary success story among animals because of their articulated limbs, chitinous covering, and occupation of many ecological niches. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 22-32)

## Arthropod emphasis

The source pays special attention to arthropods and then to insects in particular. Insects are treated as extraordinarily diverse and ecologically important, while arachnids, crustaceans, and myriapods are used to show how body appendages and body regions support class-level distinctions within the phylum. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 22-32)
