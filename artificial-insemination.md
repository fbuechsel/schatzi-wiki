# Artificial Insemination

**Summary**: Cross-species summary of how the zootechnics source presents artificial insemination and artificial fertilisation as livestock reproduction tools.

**Sources**: raw/Zootechnics.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-14

---

The source presents artificial insemination as a practical livestock technique for improving reproduction management, extending the use of valuable breeding stock, reducing direct mating, and controlling some disease risks. It discusses the technique most fully for cattle, but also mentions sheep, horses, pigs, and mule breeding. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 23, 35-36, 45, 50, 59)

## Cattle

In cattle, artificial insemination is described as time-sensitive because fertilisation depends on the overlap between egg survival and sperm survival. The source says the egg survives for about 8 to 12 hours if not fertilised and spermatozoa survive about 40 hours, making the timing of insemination important. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 23)

The source describes the cervical-rectal method as introducing one hand into the rectum to locate the cervix while the other hand deposits semen into the cervix with a tube mounted on a gun-like instrument. It gives an optimum insemination interval of 8 to 24 hours after heat begins. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 23)

For cattle breeding, the source emphasizes three advantages: genetic improvement through wider use of good breeder stock, economic efficiency because one ejaculation can be diluted to inseminate many females, and health protection because infectious genital diseases are less likely to spread without direct mating contact. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 23)

## Sheep

In sheep, the source presents artificial insemination mainly in the context of heat synchronization. It describes vaginal sponges impregnated with synthetic progestin, left in place for 7 to 9 days, followed by ram introduction or two artificial inseminations at 48 and 72 hours after sponge removal. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 35-36)

## Horses and Equid Hybrids

For horses, the source says artificial fertilisation can work well from zootechnical and health perspectives. It describes semen collection with an artificial vagina, cooling to 25 degrees C, dilution, storage at 3 to 5 degrees C for 24 hours, and insemination by syringe. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 45)

For mules, the source recommends artificial fertilisation because mating mares and donkeys is difficult and natural mating has relatively low fertility. It says artificial fertilisation increases fertility compared with natural mating. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 50)

## Pigs

In pigs, the source says artificial insemination is widespread in Italy and has health and economic advantages. It states that one boar ejaculation can provide 10 to 14 doses, allowing the same number of sows to be impregnated while reducing infection risks associated with direct mating. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 59)

## Source-Specific Limits

This page summarizes the source's practical account and should not be treated as a current procedural manual. Species-specific insemination protocols, disease testing, animal welfare rules, semen handling standards, and who may legally perform the procedure vary by jurisdiction and require current veterinary guidance. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 23, 45, 59)
