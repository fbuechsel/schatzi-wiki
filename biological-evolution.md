# Biological Evolution

**Summary**: Overview of the source's transition from fixism to Lamarck, Darwin, and branching evolutionary history.

**Sources**: raw/Zoology.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-14

---

The source contrasts fixism, which treats species as unchanged since creation, with evolutionary theories that explain change over time. It frames this shift as one of the major conceptual changes in biology and treats evolutionary thinking as essential for understanding both classification and the history of life. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 43-50)

## Fixism and Lamarck

Fixism in the source is the belief that the species present today are the same species originally created and that the natural world forms a stable order rather than a history of transformation. The book uses this as the backdrop against which later evolutionary theories become intelligible. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 43-50)

Lamarck is presented as the first major thinker to propose a coherent transformist account. The source emphasizes environmental pressure, organ use and disuse, adaptation, and inheritance of acquired characters, with the giraffe's neck serving as the classic illustrative case. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 43-50)

## Darwin and natural selection

Darwin's theory is presented as more strongly evidence-based and tied to fossils, geology, biogeography, and analogies with artificial selection practiced by breeders. The core mechanism in the source is natural selection operating on variable offspring in environments where not all individuals survive and reproduce. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 43-50)

The text stresses two components of Darwin's view: variability arises among offspring, and the environment favors some of those variants over others. Unlike Lamarck, Darwin's explanation does not rely on an internal striving toward improvement, but on differential survival and reproduction across long spans of time. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 43-50)

## Branching history and fossils

The source repeatedly insists that evolution should not be imagined as a ladder from lower to higher forms. Instead it uses the image of a branching tree, in which many lineages diverge, some persist, and others become extinct without leaving descendants that survive to the present. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 43-50)

Fossils are treated as important but incomplete evidence. The book warns that fossil forms may represent side branches rather than direct ancestors and uses both horse evolution and the relation between humans and apes to caution against oversimplified linear stories. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 43-50)

## From Darwinism to synthesis

The closing part of the chapter describes Darwinism as a broader scientific current that later absorbed results from genetics. The “synthetic theory of evolution” is presented as the union of Darwinian natural selection with heredity, allowing the accumulation of many small changes over long periods to explain major biological transformation. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 43-50)
