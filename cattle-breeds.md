# Cattle Breeds

**Summary**: Source-specific overview of cattle breeds used for milk, meat, or mixed production, with emphasis on Italian and internationally important breeds.

**Sources**: raw/Zootechnics.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-14

---

The source organizes cattle breeds mainly by production goal: dairy breeds, meat breeds, and dual-purpose or historically triple-purpose breeds. It uses breed morphology, origin, distribution, hardiness, production capacity, and meat or milk quality as the main comparison criteria. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 3-18)

## Dairy and Dairy-Leaning Breeds

The Italian Friesian is presented as a major dairy breed descended from Dutch and North American stock and associated in Italy with the Po Valley. The source describes black-pied or red-pied coat variants, strong udder attachment, prominent abdominal veins, centered teats, adult female weight from 550 to 900 kg, adult male weight from 900 to 1300 kg, and a breed-card average pregnancy length of 287 days. This breed-card value differs from the general cattle pregnancy average of 283 days summarized on [[cattle-reproduction-and-lactation]]. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 5, 24)

Brown cattle are described as hardy dairy animals with brown coat coloration and strong adaptability. The source distinguishes Brown Mountain cattle from Brown Swiss cattle, connecting the former to southern Germany and the latter to the Swiss canton of Schwyz, while describing both as useful in difficult environments and more milk-oriented than meat-oriented in the source's account. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 6-7)

The Jersey breed is presented as a small dairy breed from the Channel Island of Jersey. The source emphasizes the high fat and protein content of Jersey milk, its small body size, fawn coat, very low meat capacity, and particular suitability for butter rather than cheese because of the thickness of the fat globules. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 8)

The Simmental breed is treated as a Swiss dual-purpose breed whose milk and meat both matter. The source describes a red-pied coat, origin in the Simme valley, adaptation to difficult pastures, milk with up to 4 percent fat, and calf growth that can reach about one kilogram per day with slaughter yield around 60 percent. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 9)

The Modican or Sardinian breed is presented as important in Sicily and hot, dry regions. The source describes a dark red adult coat, hardiness, cost-effectiveness, moderate milk production under poor feeding conditions, roughly 4 percent milk fat, poor meat production, and use in crossing to produce the Sarda Red. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 10)

## International Meat Breeds

The Hereford is described as an English meat breed with red coat and white head, strong grazing suitability, resistance to infectious and parasitic diseases, and abundant high-quality meat that can contain excessive fat. The source says it is widespread in the United States and Argentina but not kept in Italy. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 11)

The Aberdeen Angus is presented as a hornless Scottish meat breed with black coat, early development, marbled meat, low stature, and a meat-animal body form. The source says it is very widespread in the United States and South America and is famous for meat quality, although it also says the meat's fat level is not acceptable to the Italian consumer. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 12-13)

The Charolais is described as a French breed selected from a former work-and-meat dual-purpose background toward meat production. The source emphasizes its creamy white coat, adaptability, use in crossing, excellent meat quality, and sensitivity to sunlight because of depigmented skin. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 13-14)

The Limousin is treated as a specialist French meat breed alongside the Charolais. The source describes a fawn coat, high slaughter yield of 63 to 65 percent, excellent carcass shape, lean meat at young age, and importance in industrial crossing for fattening animals. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 15)

## Italian Meat Breeds

The Chianina is described as one of the oldest cattle breeds and as the largest known cattle breed in the source's account. The source connects it to the Tiber and Chiana valleys, says adult bulls can reach up to 2 meters at the withers and exceed 1700 kg, and emphasizes porcelain-white coat, somatic gigantism, meat quality, and the dorsal-lumbar cuts used for Florentine steak. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 16)

The Marchigiana is presented as a breed derived from crossing Podolica cattle with Chianina cattle and as the third meat breed in Italy. The source describes a grey-white coat, fawn calves until 4 to 6 months, good meat marbling and tenderness, low milk production for the calf, and a shift from former milk-and-work use to meat use. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 17)

The Piedmontese breed is described as a former triple-purpose breed reshaped toward meat and milk after the decline of work use. The source links it to Zebu genetic contribution, emphasizes the "double-rump" muscular condition, top-quality beef, and veal production from animals slaughtered around 15 to 18 months at 500 to 600 kg live weight. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 18)
