# Cattle Farming

**Summary**: Overview of the source's treatment of cattle husbandry, including production categories, body assessment, rearing systems, and the relation between dairy and meat production.

**Sources**: raw/Zootechnics.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-14

---

The source presents cattle farming as one of the oldest and economically most important livestock activities because of the number of animals raised and the quantity of products supplied. It states that domestic cattle belong to the species *Bos taurus*. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 3)

## Production Orientation

The cattle chapter divides Italian cattle into dairy or meat breeds and combined dairy-meat or meat-dairy breeds, depending on which product takes precedence. It notes that working breeds are not treated because they had largely lost agricultural importance after mechanization, although such animals had historically been called triple-purpose because they produced meat, milk, and work. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 3)

Dairy-type cattle are described by the source as having an extensive digestive tract, narrow head, large mammary system, well-attached udders, and four proportionate teats. The source uses the image of a rectangular trapezium to describe the overall dairy-cow form. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 3)

Meat-type cattle are described through muscular development, rump and thigh formation, slaughter yield, early development, and carcass quality. The source repeatedly contrasts meat breeds with dairy breeds by emphasizing muscle mass, skeletal lightness, fat or lean meat, and suitability for grazing or crossing. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 11-18)

## Milk in the Cattle Chapter

The source defines milk as the product obtained from regular, complete, uninterrupted milking of the udder of a healthy and well-nourished animal, and it says that the unqualified term "milk" refers to cow milk unless another mammal is specified. It also notes that milk follows colostrum after birth and that mammary-gland number differs by species. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 3-4)

Milk composition is treated comparatively across mammals. The table in the source compares human, cow, goat, donkey, buffalo, and sheep milk by water, lipids, proteins, lactose, vitamins, and minerals, and the surrounding text stresses that composition can vary both between species and within a species over time. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 4)

## Animal Categories

The source gives separate cattle terminology for reproductive animals and animals destined for fattening. Reproductive terms include calf, young heifer, heifer, pregnant heifer, cow, young bull, and bull, while fattening terms include suckling calf, fattening calf, light bullock, and heavy bullock. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 19)

Those categories connect age, sex, reproductive status, diet, and slaughter target. For example, a cow is defined as a female that has given birth at least once, while a suckling calf is fed only milk and slaughtered at about four months for white meat. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 19)

## [[zoognostics]] and Body Regions

The source uses [[zoognostics]] for external body assessment. It divides the animal into head, neck, torso, front legs, and rear legs, then subdivides these into named zoognostic regions such as sinciput, forehead, horn, withers, brisket, rump, fetlock, pastern, stifle, and hock. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 20)

This vocabulary matters because breed descriptions and production evaluations depend on visible body form. The source links withers to stature or height, shin to metacarpals and metatarsals, fetlock to metacarpal and metatarsal phalangeal joints, and hock to the lower tibia epiphysis and tarsal joint. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 20)

## Rearing Systems

The source distinguishes wild, loose-stall, and cubicle-stall systems for cattle. It presents grazing as natural and potentially useful for forage resources that are hard to exploit otherwise, but says year-round fresh fodder makes the system difficult to apply broadly in Italy except in contexts such as Maremma with hardy Maremmana cattle. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 21)

Loose stalls are described as historically common and labor-intensive, while cubicle stall systems are described as more recent and widespread for both meat and milk cattle. This makes [[livestock-breeding-systems]] one of the practical links from cattle husbandry to sheep and pig management. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 21)
