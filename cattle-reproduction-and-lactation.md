# Cattle Reproduction and Lactation

**Summary**: Source-specific account of cattle puberty, oestrus, fertilisation, artificial insemination, pregnancy, birth, postpartum care, colostrum, and lactation.

**Sources**: raw/Zootechnics.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-14

---

The source treats reproduction as a practical management system that begins with puberty but does not equate puberty with readiness for breeding. It says animals become pubescent when reproductive organs have fully developed, while the age of first breeding depends on sex, breed, constitution, maturity, and production goal. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 21)

## Puberty and Heat

Male cattle are described as showing sexual instinct by vocalizing, approaching and mounting animals, and attacking other males when females in heat are present. Female heat signs include swelling and reddening of the vulva and vaginal mucosa, clear stringy mucus sometimes streaked with blood, bellowing, mounting attempts, restlessness, and reduced appetite. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 21)

The cow is described as polyoestral, with heat manifestations recurring about every three weeks. The source divides the cattle oestrous cycle into proestrus of 3 to 4 days, oestrus of about 1 day, metoestrus of 2 to 3 days, and dioestrus of 14 to 15 days. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 21-22)

## Breeding Regime

The source says bulls generally begin reproductive use at 15 to 18 months, with variation by age, sexual maturity, and constitution. It gives practical limits of 60 to 70 females in the first and second years, 100 to 150 females in adults, 1 to 2 covers per day at most, and intervals that limit use to about 4 to 5 covers per week. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 22)

Dairy heifers are said not to be mated before 12 to 14 months and should weigh at least 320 to 350 kg. Dairy cows are described as usually mated 40 to 60 days after giving birth, preferably after the second post-birth heat, while the source says good dairy cows may remain economically useful until after the eighth or ninth lactation and beef cows until around 10 years if calving interval remains under 15 months. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 22)

## Fertilisation and Artificial Insemination

The source defines fertilisation as the meeting of egg and spermatozoon and the fusion of nuclei and genetic material. It says the egg reaches the upper third of the oviduct about 6 hours after ovulation and survives for about 8 to 12 hours if not fertilised, while spermatozoa survive about 40 hours. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 23)

[[artificial-insemination]] is described as time-sensitive, with the source giving an optimum insemination interval of 8 to 24 hours after heat begins. It describes the cervical-rectal method as introducing one hand into the rectum to locate the cervix while the other deposits semen into the cervix using a tube mounted on a gun-like instrument. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 23)

The source emphasizes artificial insemination for genetic, economic, and health reasons. It says semen preservation and transport can extend the progeny of valuable breeders, progeny testing can evaluate breeding stock more rapidly, one bull can inseminate over a thousand cows in a year, and the absence of direct mating can reduce transmission of infectious genital diseases. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 23)

## Embryo Transfer, Pregnancy, and Birth

Embryo transfer is described as moving embryos from a donor cow to recipient cows so valuable breeding animals can produce more offspring during their reproductive life. The source says embryos can be frozen, stored, moved over large distances, and transplanted with techniques similar to artificial fertilisation. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 23)

Pregnancy in cattle is said to last a general average of 283 days. The source also gives breed-card values elsewhere, such as 287 days for the Italian Friesian, so the 283-day value should be read as a broad cattle average rather than a breed-specific rule. The source lists practical external signs as interruption of seasons, character change, increased appetite, and udder-volume increase from the third month. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 5, 24)

The source describes cattle twins as either monozygotic or dizygotic and explains freemartin sterility when different-sex dizygotic twins share fetal circulation and male hormones interfere with female development. It describes birth in three phases: preparatory or dilation period, expulsion period, and afterbirth period. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 24)

## Postpartum Care and Lactation

After birth, the source says the cow needs quiet, hygienic bedding, protection from drafts, and preferably a birthing room in cubicle-stall systems. It says the newborn calf should be cleared of mucus, supported with artificial respiration if asphyxiated, and dried by the mother or by hand. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 25)

The source links lactation to endocrine control by prolactin and progesterone. It says progesterone rises during pregnancy and inhibits prolactin's entry into circulation, while after birth progesterone formation stops and prolactin enters circulation, triggering milk synthesis in mammary alveoli. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 25)

[[colostrum]] is described as the mammary secretion of the first 24 hours after birth, followed by transition milk between 24 and 72 hours. The source says the cow's epitheliochorial placenta does not allow antibodies to pass from mother to fetus, so passive immunity must be transferred through colostrum early after birth. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 25)

The source sets traditional cattle lactation at 305 days, with milk production rising until days 30 to 40, remaining steady for about two months, and then declining before a dry period. It says production depends on factors such as age, breed, health, food quality, and number of lactations, and that milking three times a day can increase total production compared with the usual two milkings. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 25-26)
