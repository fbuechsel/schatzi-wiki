# Colostrum

**Summary**: Source-specific account of colostrum as the first mammary secretion after birth and its role in passive immunity for calves.

**Sources**: raw/Zootechnics.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-14

---

The source defines colostrum as the secretion produced by mammary glands in the first 24 hours after birth. It distinguishes colostrum from transition milk, which it places between 24 and 72 hours after birth. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 25)

## Composition

The source says colostrum has higher protein and lipid content than ordinary milk and lower lactose content. It treats these compositional differences as part of the immediate postpartum support system for the newborn calf. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 25)

## Passive Immunity

The source says the cow has an epitheliochorial placenta, which does not allow antibodies to pass from mother to fetus during pregnancy. For that reason, the calf receives passive immunity through colostrum rather than through the placenta. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 25)

The source warns that if colostrum is not administered properly in the early hours after birth, the calf can lack antibodies and become predisposed to many diseases. It says colostrum can be taken directly from the teat or administered by bottle-feeding. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 25)

## Digestive Role

The source also says colostrum has an effective laxative action that helps clear meconium, the waste material accumulated in the fetal digestive tract during intrauterine life. This makes colostrum part of both immune and digestive transition after birth. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 25)

## Source-Specific Limits

This page preserves the source's general husbandry account. Current calf-care protocols may specify timing, quality testing, volume, hygiene, and disease-control standards that are not detailed in the source and should be verified through current veterinary guidance. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 25)
