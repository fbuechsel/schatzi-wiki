# Donkeys, Mules, and Hinnies

**Summary**: Source-specific account of donkeys and equid hybrids, including classification, Italian donkey breeds, mule and hinny parentage, sterility, breeding, use, and management.

**Sources**: raw/Zootechnics.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-14

---

The source treats donkeys, mules, and hinnies as part of the horse-breeding chapter because they are equids used for work, carrying, pulling, milk, landscape management, and other practical roles. It classifies donkeys as mammals in Perissodactyla, family Equidae, genus *Equus*, and species *Equus asinus*. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 47)

## Donkeys

The source says donkeys and their hybrids experienced renewed interest after a period of crisis because they are docile, cost-effective, hardy, and usable in several lower-cost roles. It lists high-value milk production, pet therapy, biological mowing along roads and highways, and guard use against coyotes on some American llama farms as examples. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 47)

The source describes donkeys as especially bred in southern Italian regions and islands, where conditions highlight their hardiness. Compared with horses, donkeys are said to have heavier heads, larger and less mobile ears, less muscular necks, short manes, less elegant overall form, and a limited coat range from white to mousy grey with common stripes and zigzags. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 47)

## Italian Donkey Breeds

The Martina Franca donkey is described as a large Puglia breed native to the areas around Martina Franca, Alberobello, Locorotondo, Ceglie Massapica, Noci, Mottola, and Massafra. The source says it was used for carrying and mule driving and describes a black coat with grey abdomen, inner thighs, and muzzle. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 47)

The Sardinian donkey is described as native to Sardinia, present on the island for centuries, lively, hardy, and useful for carrying and agricultural work. The source describes a mousy grey coat, stripe, dark border on the ears, and sometimes zebra stripes on the back and legs. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 48)

The Asinara donkey is described as native to the island of Asinara in Sardinia, small like the Sardinian donkey, and marked by a white coat probably due to incomplete albinism. The source says about 120 lived wild on the island and describes pink nose and pink or pale blue eyes. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 48)

## Mules and Hinnies

The source defines a mule as the hybrid from a male donkey and a mare, and a hinny as the hybrid from a stallion and a female donkey. It says the two hybrids differ despite having the same genetic heritage because of cytoplasmic heredity. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 49)

The mule is described as more horse-like in body and more donkey-like in legs, less prone to illness than the horse, less demanding in management, and more valued than the hinny. The source says mules and hinnies are usually sterile, though female mules may occasionally be fertile and may give birth to live young. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 49)

The source explains hybrid sterility in relation to chromosome number, giving 64 chromosomes for *Equus caballus* and 62 for *Equus asinus*. It also says horse-zebra hybrids can be found in zoos and circuses but treats them as zoological curiosities rather than farm animals. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 49)

## Mule Management

The mule is valued in the source for robust constitution, hardiness, disease resistance, adaptation to unfavorable and inaccessible regions, and moderation. It is described as useful for pulling and carrying, especially on difficult mountain roads. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 49-50)

The source recommends artificial fertilisation to overcome mating difficulties between mares and donkeys and to improve fertility over natural mating, which it gives as 30 to 50 percent. It says male mules are castrated around one and a half years, training for work usually occurs at 18 to 24 months, full performance arrives around 4 years, and some working mules live 30 to 50 years. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 50)
