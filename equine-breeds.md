# Equine Breeds

**Summary**: Source-specific overview of selected horse breeds: Arab thoroughbred, Berber, English thoroughbred, and Quarter Horse.

**Sources**: raw/Zootechnics.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-14

---

The source's horse-breed survey focuses on breeds valued for riding, sport, racing, endurance, light pulling, and breed improvement. It emphasizes origin, morphology, temperament, coat color, speed, staying power, and influence on later breeds. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 39-44)

## Arab Thoroughbred

The Arab thoroughbred is described as originating in Arabia among Bedouin tribes, where scarce water and pasture led to close human contact and harsh selection conditions. The source says it is used globally for purity and cross-breeding because of speed and strength, and that it has also been used for racing in Italy. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 39)

The source describes Arab qualities as harmony, elegance, vivacity, excellent staying power, strength, riding aptitude, speed surpassed only by the English thoroughbred, and aptitude for light pulling. It gives subtypes and related categories such as Bedouin Arab, thoroughbred Arab, and broader Arab breed horses of eastern blood. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 40)

Morphologically, the Arab is described as mesomorphic, noble, harmonious, and correct in movement, with grey, bay, sorrel, black, and rarer roan coats. The source gives height at the withers from 145 to 160 cm and weight from 350 to 450 kg. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 40)

## Berber Horse

The Berber, also called the "Barbarian" horse, is described as North African and linked to Algeria, Morocco, Tunisia, and Libya. The source says Berber blood contributed to famous equine breeds and may have contributed to the English thoroughbred. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 41)

The source distinguishes the Berber from the Arab by a longer head with a slightly convex profile and longer robust legs with pronounced lean tendons. It describes the breed as resistant to climate variation, fatigue, and disease, as late developing, and as vigorous, courageous, docile, fast, enduring, and cost-effective. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 41)

## English Thoroughbred

The English thoroughbred is described as the proper "Thoroughbred" or "Race Horse" and as a dolichomorphic type considered the best galloper in the world. The source says it was developed about three centuries earlier by crossing Arab thoroughbred and Berber blood with English indigenous horses. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 42)

The source identifies Darley Arabian, Byerley Turk, and Godolphin Arabian or Godolphin Barb as founding stallions in the breed's origin story. It describes current types as stayer, sprinter, and intermediate, with uses in flat racing, hurdles, jumping, and breed improvement. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 42)

The English thoroughbred is described as having full coat colors such as bay, sorrel, less common black and grey, and exceptional roan, with distinctive forehead and leg markings valued. The source gives height from 147 to 178 cm, average height around 163 cm, weight from 320 to 450 kg, great speed, excellent staying power, and a temperament requiring expert sensitive riders. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 42)

## Quarter Horse

The Quarter Horse is described as compact, solid, heavily muscled, and associated with conformation shows, work with cattle, reining, racing bloodlines, and athletic specialization. The source gives height at the withers from 150 to 165 cm and weight from 430 to 550 kg in the summary features. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 43)

The source emphasizes the Quarter Horse's muscular back and rump, long sharply angled rump, ability to stop or change direction quickly, powerful chest and front legs, and gait useful for traveling long distances comfortably. It connects these traits to the breed's history as a herder's work horse and later competition horse. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 43-44)

The source lists recognized AQHA coat colors including bay, black, brown, sorrel, chestnut, dun, red dun, grullo, buckskin, palomino, grey, red roan, and blue roan. This unusually detailed coat list makes the Quarter Horse section more visually descriptive than the other horse-breed entries. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 44)
