# Goat Rearing

**Summary**: Source-specific notes on goat classification, domestication, morphology, production goals, and Italian breed groupings.

**Sources**: raw/Zootechnics.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-14

---

The source places goat rearing after the sheep chapter and treats goats as a related but distinct livestock topic. It says goats are ruminant cavicorn mammals in the order Artiodactyla and species *Capra hircus*. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 37)

## Classification and Domestication

The source says artiodactyls have an even number of toes, with the two inner toes carrying body weight and the two outer toes poorly developed. It also says goats were among the first domesticated animals after dogs and were certainly the first domesticated ruminants in its account. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 37-38)

The source states that Europe has no wild goat population except in Greece and mentions feral populations in Italy, including Montecristo's goat. This statement should be treated as source-specific and may need verification for current conservation or taxonomy use. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 38)

## Morphology Compared With Sheep

The source distinguishes goats from ewes by long hair that is not very wavy, a beard under the chin, and cutaneous appendices at the side of the neck called flaps. These visible traits are used as practical husbandry differences rather than as a full anatomical comparison. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 38)

## Production Goals

The source says goats are raised in tropical and subtropical countries for meat, fibers, and skin, while the Western world mainly raises goats for milk. It emphasizes that goats adapt well to intensive stall farming and mechanical milking, which makes them relevant to applied dairy management. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 38)

The source also says goat numbers in Italy are lower than sheep numbers at a ratio of about 1 to 10, but had increased in recent years because high-value cheeses were meeting market demand. This statement reflects the source's publication context rather than a current census. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 37)

## Italian Breed Groupings

The source groups goat breeds reared in Italy into Alpine breeds, Mediterranean breeds, and Asiatic breeds. It lists Saanen, Camosciata delle Alpi, and Toggenburg among Alpine breeds, and then lists native Alpine regional breeds from Valle d'Aosta, Piedmont, Lombardy, Trentino Alto Adige, and Friuli Venezia Giulia. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 38)

For Mediterranean breeds, the source lists Garganica, Maltese, Ionica, Sarda, Girgentana, and Rossa Mediterranea, also described as derived from Syria. The source does not expand the Asiatic category on the extracted page, so this page should not infer details beyond the source's list. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 38)
