# Horse Breeding

**Summary**: Overview of the source's horse-breeding material, including equine classification, Italian production context, selected breeds, reproduction, pregnancy, and birth.

**Sources**: raw/Zootechnics.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-14

---

The source presents horse breeding as an activity with changing economic functions. It says working horses had different roles by species and breed, while recent decades had made meat production more important, and it distinguishes northern Italian draft use from southern and island use for riding, sport, and light pulling. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 39)

## Equine Classification

The source classifies horses as mammals in the order Perissodactyla, family Equidae, genus *Equus*, and species *Equus caballus*. It relates horses to zebras, onager donkeys, domestic donkeys, mules, and hinnies. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 39)

Perissodactyls are described as ungulates with an odd number of toes, where the middle toe is more developed and supports body weight. This classification links the horse chapter to broader [vertebrates](vertebrates.md) and [animals](animals.md) pages, while the source itself keeps the discussion practical and breed-centered. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 39)

## Breeds and Uses

The source covers the Arab thoroughbred, Berber horse, English thoroughbred, and Quarter Horse. These breeds are treated through origin, morphology, coat colors, height, weight, temperament, speed, endurance, riding use, racing use, and influence on other breeds. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 39-44)

The breed material is summarized separately in [equine-breeds](equine-breeds.md), because the horse chapter gives enough detail for breed comparison and because the source later shifts from breed description to reproductive management. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 39-45)

## Reproduction

The source says stallions and mares first come into heat at about two years of age, with mating generally occurring between 2 and 3 years depending on breed and development. It describes the mare as a seasonal polyoestrous animal with an average 22-day ovarian cycle and visible oestrus lasting 4 to 8 days. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 45)

Mares are said to go into heat between March and June, which the source identifies as the best mating period because foals born after 330 to 350 days of pregnancy arrive during spring pasture. Heat signs in the mare include restlessness, listlessness, frequent urination, vulvar swelling, redness of vaginal mucosa, vulvar contractions, clitoral exposure, and spraying urine. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 45)

Horse mating can be free or by hand in the source's account. In free mating, one stallion runs with 30 to 40 mares, while hand mating brings a mare in heat to the stud and usually covers her on days 3 and 5 of heat and, if needed, day 7. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 45)

Artificial fertilisation is also described for horses. The source says semen is collected with an artificial vagina, cooled to 25 degrees C, diluted, held at 3 to 5 degrees C for 24 hours, and used for fertilisation through inoculation with a 20 to 30 cc syringe. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 45)

## Pregnancy and Birth

The source gives average mare pregnancy as 340 to 345 days and donkey pregnancy as about 380 days. It describes observational pregnancy diagnosis, veterinary vaginal-uterine examination, rectal palpation, urinalysis, and ultrasound. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 45-46)

Birth in mares is described as broadly similar to cattle but usually faster, with dystocia described as rare. The source recommends a groom's presence, clearing mucus and fetal-sac residue from the foal, artificial respiration if signs of asphyxia are present, washing the mare's tail and vulva, and giving warm mash after birth. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 46)
