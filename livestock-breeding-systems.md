# Livestock Breeding Systems

**Summary**: Cross-species overview of the source's livestock rearing systems, including grazing, stall systems, transhumance, permanent facilities, free-range pigs, outdoor-bred pigs, and indoor industrial systems.

**Sources**: raw/Zootechnics.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-14

---

The source treats rearing systems as practical compromises among environment, labor, cost, animal hardiness, product quality, and available feed. It compares cattle, sheep, and pigs most explicitly, while goat and equid sections add smaller management notes. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 21, 34, 37-38, 52-53)

## Cattle Systems

For cattle, the source distinguishes wild grazing, loose stalls, and cubicle stalls. It presents grazing as natural and economically interesting where forage resources are otherwise hard to exploit, but it says the system requires fresh fodder throughout the year and is therefore difficult to use broadly in Italy. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 21)

Loose stalls are described as a system formerly used widely for cattle of any breed and purpose, especially on small dairy farms with up to 30 to 50 productive animals, but the source says labor cost makes it unattractive for most uses. Cubicle stalls are described as a more recent system now widespread for both meat and milk cattle. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 21)

## Sheep Systems

The sheep chapter describes great [transhumance](transhumance.md) as a nomadic system in which flocks of 300 to more than 2000 sheep moved hundreds of kilometers along sheep-tracks between lowland and mountain pastures. The source says this practice declined because of fewer shepherds, loss of sheep-tracks and road traffic, and falling profitability from smaller flocks. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 34)

Short [transhumance](transhumance.md) is described as reduced but still present where pastures sit directly above farms and flocks are small. The source presents it as worth preserving because it supports traditional cheese and dairy products with good returns to shepherds. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 34)

Intensive open-air farming with fences is described as rotational grazing through fenced plots, with stocking based on forage production and grazing duration. The source says this system lets one shepherd supervise flocks of 200 to 300 sheep, supplement feed when pasture is insufficient, and provide water where wells or springs are absent. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 34)

Permanent sheep facilities are described as increasingly common in several European countries because of difficulty finding shepherds. The source says this system can rear many sheep even on small farms if water is sufficient to grow fodder, and it gives a shelter standard of about 2 square meters per ewe and lamb for high-number operations. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 34)

## Goat Systems

The goat section says goats are raised for meat, fibers, and skin in tropical and subtropical countries, while the Western-world objective is mainly milk. It identifies two management-relevant traits: goats adapt well to stall farming and to mechanical milking. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 38)

## Pig Systems

The pig chapter says Italian pig farming now mainly consists of industrial systems, while family farms and smallholdings have largely disappeared. It distinguishes closed cycle farms, open reproduction cycle farms, and open fattening cycle farms for heavy pigs. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 51-52)

Free-range pigs are described as living outdoors all year and eating, mating, and reproducing in the open. The source presents advantages in economic terms, meat quality, and ecology, because pigs consume undergrowth resources, move continuously, produce savory meat, help clean undergrowth, and avoid some manure-disposal problems found in stall farms. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 52)

Outdoor-bred pig farming is described as a mixed system in which animals stay in a barn or pigsty, receive an initial ration, and graze during favorable periods. The source says this system is especially suited to pregnant females and growing piglets because sunlight and movement support healthier, quicker development with lower production costs. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 52)

Indoor pig farming is described as the most widespread system in Italy and the source's main explanation for high pig-production yields. The source also flags environmental problems from manure and sewage disposal, poor air quality around large complexes, and reliance on feed and fodder brought from outside the farm. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 52-53)
