# Livestock Reproduction Techniques

**Summary**: Cross-species summary of the source's practical reproduction guidance for cattle, sheep, horses, pigs, and equid hybrids.

**Sources**: raw/Zootechnics.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-14

---

The source treats reproduction as a central livestock-management problem that combines puberty, timing of first use, detection of heat, choice of natural or [[artificial-insemination]], pregnancy management, birth, and postpartum care. Its most detailed sections concern cattle, sheep, horses, and pigs, with mules and hinnies treated through hybrid breeding. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 21-26, 35-36, 45-46, 49-50, 59)

## Cattle

Cattle reproduction is described through a polyoestrous cycle recurring about every three weeks, practical heat signs, male and female breeding schedules, fertilisation timing, cervical-rectal [[artificial-insemination]], embryo transfer, pregnancy, twin types, birth, postpartum care, [[colostrum]], and lactation. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 21-26)

Artificial insemination is especially important in the cattle section. The source says it supports genetic improvement, progeny testing, economical use of semen, and reduced transmission of infectious genital diseases because mating contact is avoided. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 23)

## Sheep

The ewe is described as a seasonal polyoestrous female with ovarian cycles in spring and autumn, though the source says some conditions can make cycles continuous through the year. It gives a 15 to 18 day average ovarian cycle, ovulation 18 to 24 hours after oestrus begins, and recommencement of heat after birth from 15 to 20 days to several weeks. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 35)

The source describes free covering as the most common method in sheep and hand covering as more logical but labor-intensive because a ewe known to be in heat is brought to the male. It also describes teaser rams, vasectomized rams, flushing to increase twin births, and synchronization of heat with progesterone or progestin-impregnated vaginal sponges followed by ram introduction or two artificial inseminations. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 35-36)

## Horses

Mares are described as seasonal polyoestrous animals with a roughly 22-day ovarian cycle and 4 to 8 days of visible oestrus. The source says mating between March and June is favored because foaling after about 330 to 350 days of pregnancy then coincides with spring pasture availability. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 45)

Horse mating can be free or by hand in the source's account. Artificial fertilisation is also described, with semen collected in an artificial vagina, cooled, diluted, stored briefly at low temperature, and inoculated with a syringe. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 45)

## Pigs

Pig reproduction is presented through breeding-stock selection, puberty, heat, and artificial insemination. The source says male and female pigs reach puberty at around 6 to 7 months, but reproductive use should be delayed until later when live weight reaches about 110 to 120 kg. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 59)

The sow is described as being in heat for 2 to 3 days, with follicles rupturing about 36 hours after oestrus begins, and as returning to heat about one week after piglet weaning. The source says artificial insemination is widespread in Italy and gives health and economic benefits because one boar ejaculation can provide 10 to 14 doses and avoid infection risks associated with direct mating. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 59)

## Equid Hybrids

The source defines a mule as the offspring of a male donkey and a mare, and a hinny as the offspring of a stallion and a female donkey. It says artificial fertilisation is recommended for mule breeding because natural mating gives poor results, and it describes the mare's pregnancy with a donkey sire as intermediate between horse and donkey gestation. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 49-50)

## Source-Specific Caution

Several procedures in the source, including tail docking, castration, dehorning, retained-placenta handling, and some assisted-birth practices, are summarized as historical or source-specific husbandry content. [[livestock-welfare-and-regulation]] separates those source descriptions from current legal, welfare, and veterinary guidance, which may differ by jurisdiction. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 25, 36, 46, 50)
