# Mendelian Genetics

**Summary**: Summary of the source's presentation of Mendel's experiments, inheritance laws, and extensions such as codominance and polygenic inheritance.

**Sources**: raw/Zoology.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-14

---

The source presents Gregor Mendel as the founder of genetics and treats his pea-plant experiments as the basis for a predictive science of heredity. It frames heredity as a fundamental property of living matter and presents Mendel's work as the point where vague ideas about resemblance between parents and offspring became a structured science. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 51-64)

## Why Mendel matters in the source

The source says Mendel's laws became especially significant after later scientists rediscovered them around 1900. It uses that rediscovery to underline the idea that Mendel had already produced a rigorous framework for heredity decades before the wider scientific community recognized its value. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 51-64)

Pea plants are treated as ideal experimental organisms because their characters can be observed clearly and because Mendel could track sharply opposed traits one by one. The source also emphasizes Mendel's use of counting and percentages, presenting statistical reasoning as part of what made his conclusions reliable. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 51-64)

## Core Mendelian framework

Mendel's central idea in the source is that hereditary characters are controlled by pairs of genes, with alternative forms called alleles. From this basis the text defines dominant and recessive characters, pure lines, hybrids, genotype, phenotype, homozygous dominant, homozygous recessive, and heterozygous combinations. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 51-64)

The source explains dominance through crosses between pure lines, then explains the reappearance of recessive traits through segregation of alleles during gamete formation and their random recombination at fertilization. These ideas are treated as the simplest model capable of explaining both similarity and variability among offspring. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 51-64)

## Mendel's laws

Three laws are stated explicitly: the law of dominance, the law of segregation, and the law of independence. The third law is especially important in the source because it allows combinations of traits to multiply rapidly, which the text uses to explain the large amount of visible variation within a species and even within a single family. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 51-64)

The source also uses independent assortment to connect genetics to everyday observation. Children can resemble their parents partly rather than completely because different combinations of hereditary factors are produced at random. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 51-64)

## Beyond simple dominance

The chapter then expands beyond two-phenotype cases. Incomplete dominance and codominance are presented as situations where heterozygotes have their own visible outcome, while ABO blood groups are used as an example of multiple alleles in which one gene pair can involve more than two possible allele forms. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 51-64)

Polygenic inheritance is introduced for traits such as height, where many gene pairs contribute to a continuous range of phenotypes. The source explicitly distinguishes this from the simpler pea-plant characters and uses it to show that heredity can generate quantitative variation rather than only sharply separated categories. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 51-64)

## Source limitations

This chapter is a useful introduction, but some of its framing is deliberately simplified relative to modern genetics. The source itself signals that polygenic inheritance is more complex than single-gene Mendelian cases, which is one reason the page includes modern follow-up references below. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 51-64)
