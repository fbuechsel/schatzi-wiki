# Monera

**Summary**: Notes on how the source describes Monera as the prokaryotic kingdom within the five-kingdom framework.

**Sources**: raw/Zoology.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-14

---

In the source, Monera are the kingdom of unicellular prokaryotes. Their defining feature is that genetic material is not enclosed in a membrane-bound nucleus, and the cell interior is described as simpler than that of eukaryotic organisms because it lacks the organelle organization found in other kingdoms. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 15-17)

## Cell organization and reproduction

The book says Monera reproduce mainly through binary fission. It describes this as duplication of the genetic filament followed by division of the cytoplasmic material into two new cells. The source also notes that many monerans possess a cell wall outside the membrane and that some species form colonies even though the individual cells remain separate organisms. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 15-17)

## How the source groups bacteria

The source focuses mainly on bacteria and groups them in several practical ways. It distinguishes aerobic from anaerobic bacteria on the basis of oxygen use, autotrophic from heterotrophic bacteria on the basis of nutrition, and parasitic from symbiotic bacteria on the basis of ecological relation to hosts. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 15-17)

Autotrophic bacteria are further divided into photosynthetic and chemosynthetic forms. The text specifically mentions iron bacteria and sulphur bacteria as examples of organisms that derive energy from chemical reactions involving inorganic substances. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 15-17)

## Ecological role

Bacteria and fungi are described as the final decomposer link in food chains because they break organic material down into simpler forms. The source also emphasizes symbiotic nitrogen-fixing bacteria of the genus *Rhizobium* and points to intestinal bacteria such as *Escherichia coli* as biologically important associates of humans and other organisms. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 15-17)

## Survival and human use

The source gives special attention to spores as resistant forms produced when conditions become unfavorable. These spores are presented as long-lived survival structures capable of enduring harsh temperature extremes until suitable conditions return. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 15-17)

Bacteria are also shown as useful to humans in fermentation, food preservation, sewage treatment, and biotechnology. Examples in the source include yoghurt and cheese production, decomposition of pollutants, and practical control of pathogenic bacteria by sterilization, disinfection, pasteurization, antibiotic chemotherapy, and vaccination. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 15-17)

This page follows the source's older kingdom terminology. For modern taxonomy, use the further-reading links on [five-kingdoms-of-nature](five-kingdoms-of-nature.md). (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 15-17)
