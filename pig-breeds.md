# Pig Breeds

**Summary**: Source-specific overview of Italian and foreign pig breeds, including Cinta Senese, Casertana, Large White, Essex, and Large Black.

**Sources**: raw/Zootechnics.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-14

---

The source says many native Italian pig breeds had become virtually extinct as more prolific foreign breeds producing more meat displaced them. It focuses on Cinta Senese and Casertana among Italian breeds and on Large White, Essex, and Large Black among foreign breeds. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 54-58)

## Cinta Senese

Cinta Senese is described as native to the hills of Siena and marked by a pale band of skin over the withers and forelimbs while the rest of the body is slate colored. The source says it is suited to free-range rearing because it is hardy, resistant, cost-effective, and a good grazer. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 54)

The source presents Cinta Senese as slow to develop and not very prolific, but valuable for tasty meat suited to high-quality sausages. It also describes crosses with Large White that produce "trimacchiati" slaughter animals combining earlier development with good meat quality. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 54)

## Casertana

The Casertana breed is described as bred in southern Italy, especially around Naples and Caserta, and as part of the Italian zootechnical heritage. The source emphasizes dark skin tending toward black, sparse or absent bristles that give the "Pelatella" name, drooping ears, short limbs, and wattles under the neck. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 55)

The source describes Casertana as one of the earliest-developing Italian breeds, able to exceed 150 kg at one year, but with limited prolificacy and fatty meat. It says the current situation was critical because only a few individuals survived and some were not thoroughbred. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 55)

## Large White

The Large White is described as an English breed from Yorkshire and as the most numerous pig breed in Italy, appearing as a thoroughbred or in crosses, especially with Landrace boars. The source says it excels in heavy-pig production but can also produce light pigs. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 56)

The source emphasizes fertility, prolificacy, maternal temperament, fast growth, good conversion index, excellent meat quality, and well-shaped hams. It gives 100 kg at 6 months, 170 to 200 kg at one year, adult boar weights up to 450 kg in one passage, and adult male weight of 300 to 350 kg in the breed card. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 56)

The Italian Large White is described as managed in the genealogical book by the National Association of Pig Breeders. The source gives white bristles, depigmented pink skin, tolerated small black or slate marks, and a sufficiently long, deep, cylindrical torso. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 57)

## Essex and Large Black

The Essex is described as similar to the Cinta Senese because of a frequent large light-colored band. The source says it is bred in England and overseas for precocity and prolificacy and is particularly suitable for bacon production. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 57)

The Large Black is described as an English breed with dark skin, black silky bristles, large hanging ears, hardiness, early development, high-quality meat, and suitability for open-air rearing in warm sunny areas because dark skin reduces susceptibility to sun rash. The source says it is the second most popular breed in England after the Large White but never became widespread in Italy. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 58)

## Role in the Source

The pig-breed chapter links breed choice to [[pig-farming]] systems. Hardy pigmented breeds are associated with outdoor or free-range conditions, while highly prolific and fast-growing foreign breeds are associated with industrial production and heavy-pig economics. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 52-58)
