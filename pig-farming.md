# Pig Farming

**Summary**: Overview of the source's treatment of pig production in Italy, including industrial concentration, heavy-pig systems, free-range and indoor farming, breed types, and reproduction.

**Sources**: raw/Zootechnics.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-14

---

The source says pig breeding increased because of demand for animal proteins and greater consumption of sausages. It presents pig farming in Italy as highly concentrated, stating that around 8 million pigs are bred each year and that about 70 percent are bred in Lombardy, Emilia-Romagna, and Veneto. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 51)

## Italian Production Context

The source says northern Italy remains oriented toward heavy-pig production because it can use relatively cheap, high-nutritional-value food such as whey from the dairy industry. It says southern regions were moving more toward light pigs using thoroughbreds and crosses with foreign breeds. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 51)

Family farms and smallholdings are described as having almost disappeared, while industrial farming is described as the main type worthy of attention in the source's context. The source contrasts older mixed farm uses of pigs with modern industrial specialization. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 51)

## Heavy-Pig Farm Types and Phases

The source lists closed cycle, open reproduction cycle, and open fattening cycle systems for heavy pigs. In closed cycle systems farms purchase only semen or boars, in open reproduction cycle systems farms purchase sows and sell fattening pigs, and in open fattening cycle systems farms purchase fattening pigs and sell fattened pigs. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 52)

The source gives key production phases as sow pregnancy of 115 days, piglet weaning at 30 days at 5 to 7 kg, return to heat about 5 days after milk-production stimulus is removed, and return to heat after 21 days if not fertilised. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 52)

## Rearing Systems

Free-range pig farming is described as keeping animals outdoors all year, where they eat, mate, and reproduce in the open. The source presents this system as economical, ecologically beneficial, and capable of producing savory meat suitable for high-quality sausages. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 52)

Outdoor-bred pig farming is described as a system in which animals stay in a barn or pigsty, receive an initial ration, and graze during favorable periods. The source says it is suited to pregnant females and growing piglets because sunlight and free movement support healthier development with lower production costs. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 52)

Indoor pig farming is described as the most widespread system in Italy and the system producing the highest number of big pigs. The source also highlights environmental problems from manure and sewage disposal, poor air around large complexes, and reliance on externally sourced feed and fodder. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 52-53)

## Evolution and Breed Types

The source classifies pigs as mammals, theria, ungulates, artiodactyls, family Suidae, genus *Sus*, and species *Sus scrofa*. It divides pig breeds into rectilinear or sub-concave, concave, and ultra-concave types based on head profile, ear position, body shape, limb length, pigmentation, hardiness, development, and meat fatness. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 53)

The rectilinear or sub-concave type is described as hardy, cost-effective, suited to grazing and strong sunlight, and marked by pigmented slate-colored skin with white spots and sparse bristles. The concave type is described as large, rounded, often depigmented, and producing meat with average fat content, while the ultra-concave type is described as early-developing and producing fatty meat. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 53)

## Reproduction

The source says breeding stock selection, especially boar selection, is central to pig-farm success and should involve morphological, genetic, functional, and health assessment. It says puberty occurs at about 6 to 7 months in both sexes, but reproductive use should be delayed until a later age and live weight of 110 to 120 kg. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 59)

Sows are described as being in heat for 2 to 3 days, with obvious signs including agitation, grunting, reduced appetite, swollen and red sexual organs, and immediate acceptance of the boar. The source says artificial insemination is widespread in Italy and provides health and economic advantages because one boar ejaculation can yield 10 to 14 insemination doses. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 59)
