# Protista

**Summary**: Summary of the source's treatment of protists as mostly unicellular eukaryotes with diverse nutrition and reproduction.

**Sources**: raw/Zoology.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-14

---

The source defines Protista as eukaryotic organisms that are mostly unicellular, although some multicellular algae are also placed in the group. It describes them as organisms that can be either autotrophic or heterotrophic and that typically live in aquatic environments or in very thin films of water. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 17-18)

## Defining features

Unlike [[monera]], protists are presented as organisms with a well-differentiated nucleus bounded by a membrane. The source emphasizes that even a single protist cell is internally complex enough to carry out all major life functions on its own. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 17-18)

The text also uses protists as an example of how classification depends on criteria. Some algae are multicellular, but the source still places them in Protista because their cells are not described as differentiated into the kinds of specialized tissues seen in higher plants and animals. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 17-18)

## Nutrition and reproduction

The source says protists may be autotrophic or heterotrophic. It also presents them as aerobic organisms whose aquatic setting can range from oceans to small wet surfaces on plants. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 17-18)

Reproduction is described as both asexual and sexual. In sexual reproduction, male and female gametes unite and combine genetic material, while in asexual reproduction a single cell divides to produce additional cells. The source also notes alternating reproductive cycles in some forms. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 17-18)

## Groups and examples

The book groups heterotrophic protists under the label “protozoa” and autotrophic ones under algae. Amoebae are used to illustrate movement by pseudopodia, while shelled planktonic protists are highlighted as important contributors to marine deposits after death. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 17-18)

Parasitic examples include *Plasmodium malariae* and *Trypanosoma gambiense*. On the autotrophic side, the source emphasizes algae as major contributors to marine plankton and names green, brown, and red algae as important multicellular groups. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 17-18)

This page reflects the source's older kingdom-level framing. For modern classification context, see [[five-kingdoms-of-nature]]. (source: Zoology.pdf - Pages 17-18)
