# Sheep Farming

**Summary**: Overview of the source's sheep-husbandry material, including Italian dairy orientation, wool decline, breed purposes, rearing systems, reproduction, and milk production.

**Sources**: raw/Zootechnics.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-14

---

The source says sheep husbandry in Italy focuses on dairy sheep for sheep-cheese production, while other countries base sheep farming more strongly on meat and wool. It therefore frames Italian sheep farming through milk, cheese, lamb, wool quality, and breed adaptation. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 27)

## Sheep Classification and Traits

The source classifies sheep as mammals, placentates, ungulates, even-toed artiodactyls, cavicorn bovids, genus *Ovis*, and species *Ovis aries*. It identifies fleece, kemp, horns, and tail as practical breed features, with horns usually present in males and rarer in females depending on breed. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 27-28)

It separates sheep breeds by production purpose into single-purpose breeds, dual-purpose breeds, and triple-purpose breeds. The named production combinations include milk, meat, wool, milk plus meat, meat plus wool, and milk plus meat plus wool. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 28)

## Wool and Dairy Context

The source describes Italian wool production as limited to sheep, while fibers from goats, camels, or rabbits are treated more generally as hair or textile fibers. It says Italian wool production declined because of synthetic fibers, rising labor costs, and competition from Australia and New Zealand, but also notes renewed interest in some Italian wool for sporting knitwear. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 27)

Sheep milk is treated primarily as a cheese-making resource. The source says milk production depends on breed, individual traits, age, number of lactations, lactation period, food, environment, and farming system, and that much sheep milk is converted into pecorino because it offers higher farmer income. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 37)

## Rearing Systems

The sheep chapter describes great [[transhumance]], short [[transhumance]], fenced open-air rotational farming, and permanent facilities. Great transhumance involved large flocks moving long distances between lowland and mountain pastures, while short transhumance persists where pastures are directly above farms. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 34)

Permanent facilities are described as increasingly common because of shepherd shortages, while fenced rotational grazing is presented as a system in which one shepherd can supervise 200 to 300 sheep. These systems connect sheep production to the broader [[livestock-breeding-systems]] page. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 34)

## Reproduction and Flock Management

The source describes ewes as seasonal polyoestrous animals, often cycling in spring and autumn, with first heat at 6 to 8 months and later puberty in rams. It says environmental conditions, breed, sex, food, temperature, breeding system, and light availability influence puberty and reproductive timing. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 35)

The source says sheep covering can be free or by hand, with free covering used most often and hand covering more controlled but more labor-intensive. It describes teaser rams, mating seasons around May-June and September-October, "Christmas lambs" and "Easter lambs," flushing to increase twin births, and hormone-based synchronization of heat. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 35-36)

Pregnancy in ewes is said to last an average of five months. The source recommends special attention to hygiene and diet from the fourth month of pregnancy and says births are usually easy and quick, with postpartum treatment similar to cattle. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 36)

## Husbandry Procedures and Identification

The source describes castration, tail docking, dehorning, marking, and electronic identification in sheep and goats. It says animals born after 31 December 2009 require an electronic identification code under cited EU regulations, while animals intended for slaughter before 12 months may use a simplified identification system. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Pages 36-37)

These handling and marking details should be read as source-specific husbandry and regulatory content. [[livestock-welfare-and-regulation]] records the caution that current rules may differ from the source's 2015 context and should be checked before applying any legal or veterinary procedure. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 37)
