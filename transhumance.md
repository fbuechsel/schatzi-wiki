# Transhumance

**Summary**: Source-specific account of great and short transhumance as sheep-rearing systems organized around seasonal movement between pastures.

**Sources**: raw/Zootechnics.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-05-14

---

The source describes transhumance as a sheep-rearing system based on moving flocks between pasture areas. It distinguishes great transhumance, which involved long-distance movement, from short transhumance, which involved smaller movements between farms and nearby higher pastures. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 34)

## Great Transhumance

Great transhumance is described as a nomadic system in which flocks of 300 to more than 2000 sheep traveled hundreds of kilometers in spring and autumn. The source says these flocks moved along sheep-tracks between lowland pastures and mountain pastures. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 34)

The source says great transhumance had fallen out of practice because shepherd numbers declined, sheep-tracks were lost or disrupted by road traffic, and herding profitability decreased as flock numbers fell. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 34)

## Short Transhumance

Short transhumance is described as much reduced but still practiced where pastures are directly above farms and flocks are small. The source presents this system as worth preserving because it supports traditional cheeses and dairy products with good profit for shepherds. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 34)

## Role in Sheep Husbandry

In the source, transhumance sits beside fenced open-air rotational farming and permanent facilities as one of the main sheep-rearing systems. It connects animal movement, pasture availability, labor, product identity, and regional tradition. (source: Zootechnics.pdf - Page 34)
